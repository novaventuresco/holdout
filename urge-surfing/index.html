The science

What is urge surfing

Urge surfing is a technique for getting through cravings without acting on them. The name comes from the idea that cravings behave like waves: they build, reach a peak, and then break on their own. The task is to ride the wave rather than fight it.

The technique comes out of relapse prevention research and has been incorporated into several evidence-based approaches to habit change. It does not require a therapist or any specialized training. It can be practiced on your own, with or without tools.

Where it comes from

Urge surfing was developed by psychologist Alan Marlatt in the 1980s as part of his work on relapse prevention. His research found that one of the main reasons people relapsed after quitting a substance was a failure to manage cravings in the moment.

The conventional advice at the time was to resist the craving, distract from it, or reason through the consequences of acting on it. Marlatt found that those strategies often backfired. Trying to suppress a craving tends to increase its intensity, a pattern sometimes called the rebound effect. The more you push an impulse away, the more forcefully it tends to return.

What worked better was a different orientation: instead of fighting the craving, observe it. Acknowledge it, watch how it moves, and wait for it to pass. This approach was later incorporated into ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) and mindfulness-based relapse prevention, where it has since been studied across multiple craving types.

The wave model

The wave metaphor works because it accurately describes the structure of a craving. A wave doesn't stop because you push back against it. Resistance gives it something to work against. But if you stop fighting and ride it, the wave carries you through.

Cravings follow a similar pattern. They build in intensity, reach a peak somewhere in the first 10 to 15 minutes, and then start to drop. The peak feels like the worst of it, and it is. But it's also the point at which the craving starts to reverse.

Most people never reach the peak before acting because the buildup feels like it will continue indefinitely. It doesn't. Research consistently shows that cravings are time-limited, and that observing a craving without acting on it tends to weaken the association between the trigger and the behavior over time.

How to practice it

There is no single prescribed method. The basic structure looks like this:

  1. When the craving hits, acknowledge it rather than trying to make it go away.
  2. Notice where you feel it physically. Cravings almost always have a physical component: tension, heat, hunger, restlessness, a pulling sensation somewhere in the body.
  3. Watch how those sensations change. The craving is not static. It shifts. Noticing the movement, rather than the discomfort itself, creates a small degree of separation between the experience and the reaction to it.
  4. Don't judge. The goal is not to make the craving disappear. It is to observe it long enough that it subsides on its own.
  5. Let it pass without acting on it. For most cravings, that is somewhere in the range of 15 to 20 minutes.

The harder part

The difficulty is that sitting with a strong craving while doing nothing is genuinely uncomfortable. The original technique assumes you can manage the observation without any external support. That is a lot to ask in the first minutes of a strong craving.

Research on behavior change generally shows that friction matters. When acting on a craving is easy and not acting on it has no support structure, most people act on it. When there is something to do during those minutes, something that gives the craving a form without requiring you to act on it, completion rates improve.

The most effective version of urge surfing tends to involve a concrete focus point. Something that makes the craving feel like an object you can watch, rather than an internal state you have to manage alone.
