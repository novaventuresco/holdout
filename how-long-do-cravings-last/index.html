The science

How long do cravings last

When a craving hits, it tends to feel like it will keep building until you give in. That feeling is real, but it isn't the full picture. Research on how cravings work suggests they follow a predictable arc: they build, peak, and then subside on their own, usually within 15 to 20 minutes.

Most people never find out because they act on the craving before the peak passes.

What's actually happening

When you experience a craving, your brain is releasing dopamine in anticipation of a reward. Dopamine is a signaling chemical tied to motivation and anticipation. The craving is that signal becoming strong enough to dominate your attention.

But dopamine doesn't stay elevated indefinitely. Like most neurochemical responses, it follows a curve. The anticipatory spike tends to peak within 10 to 15 minutes and then fall off, even if you don't act on it.

This pattern has been observed across different types of cravings: food, nicotine, alcohol, and behavioral impulses. The window varies between individuals and circumstances, but 15 to 20 minutes covers the range for most people under most conditions.

Why it doesn't feel like it will pass

The craving feels permanent partly because of how the mind handles discomfort. When something is urgent and unpleasant, the instinct is to resolve it as quickly as possible. Acting on the craving is the fastest path to relief, so most people take it without waiting to see whether the craving would have passed on its own.

There's also a reinforcement pattern at work. Every time you act on a craving, you confirm to your brain that acting on it is how the discomfort ends. Over time, the association between the craving and the behavior gets stronger, which is part of what makes habitual cravings hard to break.

None of this is a moral failing. It's just how the mechanism works. Knowing the structure of it is what makes it possible to do something different.

The 20-minute window

The 20-minute window isn't an arbitrary number. It comes from research on urge surfing, a technique developed by psychologist Alan Marlatt in the 1980s as part of his work on relapse prevention. His finding was that cravings behave like waves: they build, crest, and then break on their own. The task isn't to fight the craving but to ride it out until it subsides.

Research on urge surfing has been carried out across smoking cessation, substance use, and food cravings. The consistent finding is that cravings are time-limited, and that people who have a way to occupy the window without acting on the craving tend to get through it at higher rates than those trying to white-knuckle it without any support.

What to do in the meantime

Knowing the craving will pass doesn't make those minutes easy. The peak is real, and sitting with it while doing nothing is uncomfortable. What changes when you understand the craving is time-limited is the framing: you are not waiting for something with no end, you are waiting for something with a known end.

Having a focus point helps. Not a distraction that pulls your attention away from the craving, but something that lets you observe it rather than react to it. Most people who practice urge surfing describe needing something concrete to look at during the peak minutes. Something that makes the craving feel like an object you can watch, rather than a state you have to manage.
